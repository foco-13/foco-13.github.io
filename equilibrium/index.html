First Law of Economics

For every economist, there exists an equal and opposite economist.

— attributed to various, proven by experience
Second Law of Economics

They are both wrong.

— attributed to various, also proven by experience
Corollary

But this long run is a misleading guide to current affairs. In the long run we are all dead. Economists set themselves too easy, too useless a task if in tempestuous seasons they can only tell us that when the storm is long past the ocean is flat again.

— J. M. Keynes, A Tract on Monetary Reform (1923)
Empirical observation

An economist is an expert who will know tomorrow why the things he predicted yesterday didn't happen today.

— Laurence J. Peter
On forecasting

The only function of economic forecasting is to make astrology look respectable.

— Ezra Solomon, Stanford economist and member of Nixon's Council of Economic Advisers
Theorem

If you put two economists in a room, you get two opinions, unless one of them is Lord Keynes, in which case you get three opinions.

— Winston Churchill (apocryphal)
Lemma

Ask five economists and you'll get five different answers — six if one went to Harvard.

— Edgar R. Fiedler, Across the Board (1977)
On prediction

Wall Street indexes predicted nine out of the last five recessions!

— Paul Samuelson, Newsweek (1966)
Field observation

Everything reminds Milton of the money supply. Well, everything reminds me of sex, but I keep it out of the paper.

— Robert Solow, in Guidelines: Informal Controls and the Market Place (1966)
Caveat emptor

The purpose of studying economics is not to acquire a set of ready-made answers to economic questions, but to learn how to avoid being deceived by economists.

— Joan Robinson, Economic Philosophy (1962)
Conjecture

If all economists were laid end to end, they would not reach a conclusion.

— attributed to George Bernard Shaw (unverified)
